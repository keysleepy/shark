Shark Information

Evolution

Sharks have been swimming in our oceans for over 450 million years, predating dinosaurs by more than 200 million years.

Anatomy

Sharks have a skeleton made of cartilage, not bone. They can have up to 3,000 teeth at once, with multiple rows of replacement teeth.

Behavior

Contrary to popular belief, most sharks are not dangerous to humans. They have complex social behaviors and some species are highly migratory.

Ecosystem Role

As apex predators, sharks play a crucial role in maintaining the health and balance of marine ecosystems.

History of Sharks

450 Million Years Ago

First shark-like fish appear in the fossil record during the Late Ordovician period.

380 Million Years Ago

Diversification of early shark species during the Devonian period, known as the "Age of Fishes."

20 Million Years Ago

Megalodon, the largest shark to ever exist, ruled the oceans. It could grow up to 60 feet long.

3.6 Million Years Ago

Megalodon becomes extinct, possibly due to cooling oceans and competition from other predators.

Present Day

Over 500 species of sharks inhabit our oceans, with many facing threats from human activities.

Shark Groups & Classification

Cartilaginous Fish (Chondrichthyes)

Sharks belong to the class Chondrichthyes, characterized by skeletons made of cartilage rather than bone. This class also includes rays, skates, and chimaeras.

Major Orders

  • Carcharhiniformes: Ground sharks (includes tiger sharks, bull sharks)
  • Lamniformes: Mackerel sharks (includes great whites, makos)
  • Orectolobiformes: Carpet sharks (includes whale sharks, nurse sharks)
  • Squaliformes: Dogfish sharks
  • Heterodontiformes: Bullhead sharks

Differences from Rays and Skates

While rays and skates are closely related to sharks, they have flattened bodies, enlarged pectoral fins, and gill slits on their underside rather than on the sides of their heads.

Many shark species are threatened by overfishing, habitat loss, and finning. Conservation efforts include protected marine areas, fishing regulations, and public education.
